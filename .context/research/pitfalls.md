# Pitfalls Research: AI-Maintained Personal Knowledge Wiki (Second Brain)

## Summary

The three risks named in the brief are real and well-documented, but they interact in a way worth naming up front: **existing implementations of the Karpathy raw/wiki pattern solve (1) decay reasonably well but do not solve (2) or (3) at all** — none of the comparable open-source implementations found (SamurAIGPT/llm-wiki-agent, Ar9av/obsidian-wiki, NiharShrotri/llm-wiki) implement a confirm/approval layer or a mechanism for an AI to push back without becoming a false authority. That means Alfonso's project is not copying a proven playbook on its most distinctive design axis (propose-then-confirm as the antidote to over-delegation) — it has to invent that part, and it should get commensurately more design scrutiny than the ingestion/lint mechanics, which do have precedent. Feature #1 (wiki structure) should treat orphan/contradiction/staleness checks as load-bearing from day one, not a later add-on; the reconciliation-loop/pushback behavior deserves the deepest first-principles design care in the whole project, precisely because it has no template to lean on.

## Findings

### 1. Wiki/knowledge-base decay over time

**Pitfall: cross-reference drift on ingest.** The single most-cited failure mode among practitioners of this exact pattern: pages get written but the index/cross-links between them are updated inconsistently, so pages "silently go stale" and become invisible to later retrieval even though they technically exist. [MEDIUM — practitioner commentary on Karpathy's own pattern, not independently verified but directly on-topic]
- *Warning sign:* `index.md` entries with no inbound/outbound links; pages only the session that wrote them "remembers" exist.
- *Prevention:* treat the index update as part of the same atomic action as the page write, never a deferred step. Lint pass must check for orphan pages every run, not opportunistically.

**Pitfall: the deep-ingestion exception is the highest-risk write path for compounding errors.** It's the one path in this system with no per-page confirm — any extraction mistake here writes directly and confidently into the wiki, unverified by Alfonso, and everything downstream (lint fill-ins, reconciliation notes, cross-venture inferences) can end up quietly built on top of it.
- *Warning sign:* multiple later pages or reconciliation notes trace back to a single deep-ingestion claim nobody has re-checked against the raw source since it was written.
- *Prevention:* apply the extracted/inferred/ambiguous trust tag strictly and immediately at ingestion time (not retrofitted later), and have lint passes treat deep-ingestion-originated pages as the first candidates for staleness/verification review, since they're the one unverified layer everything else is allowed to build on. [LOW-MEDIUM — reasoned from the project's own stated design plus general knowledge-base practice, not a specific external source]

**Pitfall: negative-value derivative pages.** One documented experiment on this exact pattern found per-entity wiki pages that ended up *larger* than the source material they summarized — forcing agents to read both the wiki page and the source, defeating the purpose of compiling knowledge once. [MEDIUM — single practitioner report, not independently verified]
- *Warning sign:* a wiki page's length approaches or exceeds the raw excerpt it's distilling; the new-page-vs-edit judgment call keeps trending toward "new page" instead of merging into an existing one.
- *Prevention:* `CLAUDE-WIKI.md`'s new-page-vs-edit rule should default toward editing/merging; reserve new pages for genuinely new, distinct, cross-linkable entities — not every fact that shows up.

**Pitfall: stale claims that nothing forces a revisit of.** This is a direct tension with the project's own "no automation on a timer, ever" constraint. Comparable implementations of this pattern resolve staleness with CI-hooked or timer-enforced lint; this project has deliberately ruled that out. That's a legitimate, already-made design choice — but it means staleness detection depends entirely on Alfonso remembering to trigger lint, with no fallback. [MEDIUM]
- *Warning sign:* a wiki page's claim is contradicted by something learned in a later ingestion or reconciliation session, but the earlier page still stands unedited months later; `log.md` shows a long gap since the last lint run with no flag anywhere calling that gap out.
- *Prevention:* since timers are out by design, anchor lint triggers to natural checkpoints already built into the workflow — end of every ingestion session, start of any new venture's work, whenever the reconciliation loop fires — rather than relying on an unprompted memory. Make "time/sessions since last lint" trivially visible (e.g., a line in `log.md` or `index.md`) so the gap is felt, not just theoretically trackable.

**Pitfall: trust tags that exist but aren't actually respected downstream.** The extracted/inferred/ambiguous tagging (per Graphify's convention, already adopted in this project's design) only protects against silent error accumulation if later reads actually treat the three tiers differently — not just store them once and never look at them again.
- *Warning sign:* pages tagged "inferred" or "ambiguous" get cited in conversation or in later wiki writes with the same confidence as "extracted" pages.
- *Prevention:* the query workflow in `CLAUDE-WIKI.md` should explicitly require surfacing the trust tag whenever a claim is cited back to Alfonso in conversation, not just when the page is opened directly.

**Pitfall: index-first discipline degrading as the index itself grows.** The design assumes reading `index.md` first is cheap; that assumption erodes if the index either grows unstructured (flat list, no sub-sectioning) or its per-entry summaries drift out of sync with what the page actually says.
- *Warning sign:* `index.md` line count growing without corresponding structure; index descriptions that no longer match current page content.
- *Prevention:* lint pass should check index-entry-vs-page-content match, not just orphan detection. Revisit index structure (e.g., sub-indexes per venture or topic) *before* flat-file scale becomes a real cost — this is explicitly a "revisit later" item, not something to solve preemptively at "hundreds of pages" scale, per the project's own scope decision. [LOW — reasoned from stated scale assumptions, not an external source]

### 2. Risks specific to "AI pushes back with independently-researched knowledge"

**Pitfall: automation bias / algorithmic authority working directly against the project's core principle.** This is the sharpest tension in the whole design. Research on algorithmic decision support consistently finds that when an AI's output is perceived as objective, well-sourced, and independent, humans *defer to it more*, not less — the opposite of what "pushback exists to make Alfonso think harder" requires. One controlled study found experienced radiologists' diagnostic accuracy dropped from 82% to 45.5% specifically in cases where the AI's suggestion was wrong and they deferred to it anyway; training and experience slowed this effect but did not prevent it. [HIGH — peer-reviewed study (Radiology, cited across multiple independent secondary sources), consistent with a broader literature on "epistemic authority" attribution to AI]
- *Warning sign:* Alfonso citing "the wiki says" or "Claude pushed back and said" as the reason for a decision, rather than his own re-derived reasoning; decisions getting made faster after pushback, with less visible deliberation than before pushback existed.
- *Prevention:* the propose-then-confirm mechanism for reconciliation/pushback should require Alfonso to articulate his own counter-reasoning, not just accept or reject Claude's framing — mirroring the "active oversight loop" pattern used in some medical-AI systems, where the system occasionally withholds its own recommendation and asks for the human's independent judgment first. The existing "grill me" interrogation tool is a good structural fit for this since it already forces answers rather than approvals — worth deliberately extending that same interrogation posture to pushback moments, not just ingestion/reconciliation capture.

**Pitfall: the AI's "independent research" becoming self-referential / unfalsifiable.** LLM-fabricated citations are a well-documented, high-frequency problem — studies report fabrication rates between roughly 18% and 69% depending on model and query type, and a well-known failure pattern is generating research that fits a predetermined conclusion after the fact rather than actually testing it. If a wiki claim written from a shaky web search gets treated as settled fact by a later lint or reconciliation pass, the system develops circular evidence: claim A is confirmed by claim B, which was itself derived from claim A. [HIGH — cross-checked across multiple sources including arXiv research on citation fabrication and reporting on real cases of fabricated academic citations propagating]
- *Warning sign:* a wiki claim's citation chain terminates in another `claude-knowledge` page rather than in `raw/`, a real external URL, or a dated conversation — the project's own citation convention (raw file / external research / "synthesized from conversation on [date]") is specifically designed to catch this, but only if lint actually checks for it.
- *Prevention:* lint pass should specifically verify that every `claude-knowledge` page's citation chain bottoms out in something outside the wiki itself. Treat "no external source confirms this yet" as a legitimate, visible trust state (this is what the ambiguous/inferred tags are for) rather than something an eager write silently smooths over.

**Pitfall: pushback-as-theater (contrarianism without substance).** The mirror image of AI sycophancy: an agent trying to "push back" can perform disagreement on low-stakes points to appear independent, while its confidence level in wording doesn't actually track how much real verification backs a given claim. This risk isn't well-documented in the literature specifically (most sycophancy research covers the opposite failure — excessive agreement), so treat this one as inference, not an established finding. [LOW — reasoned extension of sycophancy research, not directly evidenced]
- *Warning sign:* pushback is consistently phrased with high confidence regardless of whether it's backed by extracted fact, inference, or a thin web search; Alfonso can't tell from the conversation alone how much verification is actually behind a given piece of pushback.
- *Prevention:* pushback delivered in conversation should carry the same trust-tag discipline the wiki itself uses — state confidence and sourcing inline when disagreeing, not just in the page that gets written afterward. This mirrors the confidence-tagging discipline used in this very research file.

### 3. Propose-then-confirm pitfalls

Note: none of the comparable open-source implementations of this pattern (SamurAIGPT/llm-wiki-agent, Ar9av/obsidian-wiki) implement a confirm/approval layer at all — they write directly and rely on lint to catch problems after the fact. This project's confirm-first design is the differentiated, higher-integrity choice, but it also means the failure modes below have no established prior art to borrow mitigations from within this specific pattern; the mitigations below are pulled from adjacent domains (human-in-the-loop AI review, content moderation, clinical decision support). [MEDIUM]

**Pitfall: confirmation fatigue / rubber-stamping from undifferentiated friction.** Reviewers who approve large volumes of AI output anchor on early items and progressively apply less scrutiny; one industry example describes reviewers "tapping approve before your eyes have finished the sentence" by the fiftieth item in a session. This is documented as empirically distinct from automation bias — it's driven by volume and repetition, not by trust in the AI's authority. [MEDIUM — industry source plus consistent findings from HITL research on decision fatigue]
- *Warning sign:* approval turnaround time shrinking session over session; multi-paragraph proposed wiki edits getting approved with zero back-and-forth or requested changes.
- *Prevention:* keep the deep-ingestion exception as the *only* no-confirm path (already the design — correct call). Within the confirm-gated paths, consider differentiating friction by stakes: a small ad-hoc conversational filing could get a lightweight diff-style summary, while a reconciliation edit that changes an existing claim's trust tag or contradicts prior content should surface fuller context before confirming. This differentiation question is worth resolving explicitly during Feature #1's discuss phase, not left implicit.

**Pitfall: the explainability paradox.** When an AI explains its reasoning alongside a proposal, humans tend to treat reading the explanation as a substitute for independently checking the claim, rather than as a starting point for scrutiny — reducing oversight instead of supporting it. [MEDIUM — single source, consistent with broader automation-bias literature]
- *Warning sign:* confirm decisions happening faster than the length of Claude's own proposal would take to actually read.
- *Prevention:* no clean technical fix exists for this; it's a discipline problem. Consider structuring proposals in `CLAUDE-WIKI.md`'s confirm workflow to force a distinct "what would make this wrong" or "what wasn't verified here" line on every proposal, prompting active evaluation rather than passive reading.

**Pitfall: accountability diffusion in a solo system.** With no second human reviewer, an incorrect wiki claim that later gets caught in reconciliation has a real risk of becoming a shrug ("the AI proposed it, I approved it") rather than a genuine post-mortem on why the original check didn't catch it.
- *Warning sign:* reconciliation entries that correct a wiki claim but `log.md` shows no trace of why the original confirmation didn't catch the error.
- *Prevention:* have `log.md` capture a one-line reason/confidence note at confirm time, not just the fact that a write happened. Cheap to add now, valuable as a debugging trail later — worth prototyping as part of Feature #1 rather than bolted on after the fact.

**Pitfall: self-inflicted batch fatigue.** Because automation-on-a-timer is permanently out of scope, there's no system-driven confirm spam — but the same fatigue pattern can still occur if Alfonso batches many real reviews into a single sitting (the same mechanism as a content moderator processing hundreds of items per shift, just self-scheduled instead of imposed).
- *Warning sign:* single sessions where ten or more proposed writes get confirmed back-to-back.
- *Prevention:* worth deciding a soft batch-size convention during Feature #1's discuss phase (e.g., flag or naturally break up a session if a single ingestion/reconciliation pass queues more than a handful of proposed writes).

## Confidence

**MEDIUM overall.** The wiki-decay findings (section 1) are well cross-sourced, including direct evidence from other implementations of this exact Karpathy pattern — HIGH-MEDIUM confidence there. The automation-bias and citation-fabrication findings (section 2) are backed by peer-reviewed or well-cross-checked research — HIGH confidence on the general phenomena, but LOW-MEDIUM confidence on how they'll specifically manifest in this project, since no comparable system implements this project's exact propose-then-confirm + pushback design. Section 3's mitigations are pulled from adjacent domains (clinical AI, content moderation) rather than this specific pattern, since no existing wiki-agent implementation has a confirm layer to study — treat those as reasoned adaptations, not proven fixes.
