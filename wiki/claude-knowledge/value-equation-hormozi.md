## The equation

Hormozi describes this as a formula he originated and hadn't seen articulated elsewhere, meant to quantify what actually creates perceived value in any offer:

> **Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)**

Two drivers you want to **increase** (numerator): the dream outcome, and the perceived likelihood of achieving it. Two drivers you want to **decrease** (denominator): the perceived time delay to achievement, and the perceived effort and sacrifice required.

**Why division, not addition — the deliberate design choice**: Hormozi says he structured it as division specifically to make one point vivid: if you can drive the denominator (time delay × effort/sacrifice) to zero, the value becomes infinite regardless of how large the numerator is, since anything divided by zero is undefined/infinite. His illustration: if a weight-loss product's effect were literally instant and effortless — click purchase, stomach becomes a six-pack immediately — or a marketing firm's phone started ringing with qualified leads the moment the contract was signed, that offer would be infinitely valuable. He's explicit no one actually reaches this limit, but frames it as the asymptote every offer should be designed to approach.

**Where he shifted focus over time**: early in his career he over-invested in the numerator (bigger dream-outcome claims, social proof, third-party edification) because it's "easy and lazy" — anyone can make a bigger promise. He states the harder, more competitive, and ultimately more valuable work is on the denominator — reducing time delay and effort/sacrifice — because those are harder to fake and harder to copy. Companies he cites as denominator-focused: Apple (made phones effortless relative to competitors at the time), Amazon (single-click purchase, near-immediate delivery), Netflix (immediate, effortless content consumption).

## Perception is reality

None of these four drivers matter as raw fact — only as the prospect's *perception* of them. Improving actual likelihood of achievement, or actually reducing time delay/effort, creates no value if the prospect doesn't perceive the change. Illustrative case: the London Underground's biggest rider-satisfaction gain didn't come from spending billions to physically speed up trains — it came from a cheap dotted map showing riders when the next train was arriving, which reduced the *perceived* time delay and the boredom/uncertainty of waiting, at a fraction of the cost of the "logical" fix.

### Psychological vs. logical solutions (attributed to Rory Sutherland, CMO of Ogilvy)

Hormozi's argument: the *logical* solution to a problem has usually already been tried, because it's the obvious move everyone reaches for — so what's left unsolved tends to be solvable only by a *psychological* solution instead. Examples given:

| Problem | Logical solution | Psychological solution |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Train wait dissatisfaction | Make trains faster | Add a countdown/dotted map showing wait time |
| Elevator wait dissatisfaction | Make elevators faster | Add floor-to-ceiling mirrors so people watch themselves and stop noticing the wait |
| Product feels too expensive | Make it cheaper | Make *fewer* of them and raise the price, which increases desire |

His framed rule: "any fool can sell a product by offering it for a discount; it takes great marketing to sell the same product for a premium." As business owners, the actionable version of this insight is to consciously look for psychological levers on the four value drivers, not just logical/operational ones.

## The four drivers in depth

### 1. Dream outcome (increase)

The dream outcome is the felt gap between a prospect's current reality and the future they've envisioned — not the mechanism, the destination. Hormozi's framing: businesses don't create desire, they *channel* pre-existing, deep human desires (the kind wars are fought over) through a specific vehicle. The real driver underneath most surface-level dream outcomes (looking beautiful, being loved, being respected) is usually **status** — relative social standing.

Illustrative anecdote (secondhand, from Russell Brunson about his wife Colette): she initially rejected the idea that status drove her choices, insisting she wasn't motivated by status because she'd never want a Lamborghini and preferred her minivan — but on reflection, realized the *reason* was itself status-driven: a Lamborghini would lower her status among other moms (looks reckless/frivolous), while the minivan signals "good mother" (raises status in that specific social context). Hormozi's generalized claim: for many men specifically, money outranks physical attractiveness as a status driver, so offers framed around making money will often out-value offers framed around looking good, for that audience.

**Copywriting pro tip he gives**: frame benefits in terms of how *other people* will perceive the prospect's achievement, not just the achievement itself. Example: "if you buy this golf club, your drive increases 40 yards — your golf buddies' jaws will drop when your ball lands 40 yards past theirs, and they'll ask what changed. Only you'll know."

**Important scoping note**: when two offers satisfy the *same* underlying desire (his example: two ways to "become beautiful"), the dream-outcome value cancels out between them — the other three drivers (perceived likelihood, time delay, effort/sacrifice) become the entire basis for why one option is worth $50,000 and another $5.

### 2. Perceived likelihood of achievement (increase)

The last driver Hormozi added to the framework — he describes initially feeling something was missing with only three, then realizing people pay specifically for *certainty*. Definition: how confident the prospect is that this specific purchase will actually get them the result.

Illustrative example: how much more would you pay to be a plastic surgeon's 10,000th patient than their first (even though the surgery itself takes the same time either way, and the more experienced surgeon might even finish faster)? The technical service is identical; only the buyer's confidence in the outcome changes, and that alone commands a large price premium. Levers for increasing this driver: messaging, proof (testimonials, data, case studies), what you choose to include or exclude in the offer, and guarantees (covered in depth on the guarantees page).

### 3. Time delay (decrease)

Two sub-components: **long-term outcome** (the actual thing being bought — e.g., an extra $239,000/year for a gym owner) and **short-term experience** (small wins along the way that keep someone engaged long enough to reach the long-term outcome). Hormozi's framing: "the thing people buy is the long-term value... but the thing that makes them stay long enough to get it is the short-term experience."

Concrete tactics he used: for Gym Launch clients, getting their ads live and landing their first $2,000 sale within the first 7 days, specifically to reinforce that signing up was the right decision early, before the real 11-month outcome ever materializes. For weight-loss clients, deliberately front-loading a more aggressive initial diet phase to produce a fast, visible early win (backed, he notes, by research showing people who experience an early victory are more likely to persist), and engineering an early social win (meeting another client) as an unplanned-but-valuable side benefit that keeps people in the program.

**"Fast beats free"**: speed itself commands a premium even against free alternatives. Examples cited: paid expedited DMV/MVD renewal vs. free-but-slow standard service; FedEx overnight vs. USPS; Spotify (paid) vs. free ad-supported/slower music access; Uber vs. walking. His rule of thumb: if you're competing against a free alternative, differentiate on speed rather than trying to compete on price.

### 4. Effort and sacrifice (decrease)

Ancillary costs beyond price itself — tangible and intangible — required to get the result. Worked comparison, fitness vs. liposuction, both selling "a leaner body":

- **Fitness**: wake 1–2 hours earlier, 5–10 hours/week lost, giving up foods you love, constant hunger, physical soreness, embarrassment at not knowing how to exercise, injury risk, nausea from working out, meal prepping, new groceries/clothing, fear of relapse.
- **Liposuction**: fall asleep, wake up thinner (guaranteed), be sore for 2–4 weeks.

Because likelihood-of-achievement, time delay, *and* effort/sacrifice are all dramatically better for the surgical route despite an identical surface-level outcome (thinner body), liposuction commands a vastly higher price than a gym membership — even though the "dream outcome" itself is comparable. Hormozi notes plastic surgeons' actual marketing copy leans directly into this: "tired of wasting hours in the gym? Tired of diets that don't work?"

**Done-for-you commands a premium over DIY for exactly this reason** — it eliminates effort/sacrifice almost entirely, and also raises perceived likelihood of achievement (an expert doing it is trusted to succeed more than the buyer doing it themselves).

## Worked comparison: meditation vs. Xanax

Both offer the same dream outcome (relaxation, decreased anxiety, well-being). Hormozi scores each driver 0 or 1 and sums for a rough relative-value index:

| Driver | Meditation | Xanax |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Dream outcome | 1 | 1 |
| Perceived likelihood | 0 (most people get distracted, don't follow through daily) | 1 (high confidence the pill works) |
| Time delay | 0.5 (long-term results take time; some immediate benefit after 10–20 min if you don't get frustrated) | 1 (effects felt in ~15 minutes) |
| Effort/sacrifice | 0 (physical/mental discomfort, feels like "failing," daily time commitment) | 1 (swallow a pill) |
| **Total** | **1.5 / 4** | **4 / 4** |

Hormozi's explicit point is *not* that Xanax is actually better for someone than meditation — he states the opposite is obviously true from a wellbeing standpoint — but that **actual goodness and perceived value are different axes**, and only perceived value drives purchasing behavior and pricing power. He extends this to a real market comparison: the global supplement industry (~$123B, per Grand View Research, as cited in the source) is roughly twice the size of the health club industry (~$62B, per HRSA, as cited in the source) despite both nominally serving the same goals (health, weight loss, looking good, energy) — because supplements score far higher on speed and low effort than a gym membership does. His stated philosophy: "you can either be right or you can be rich... know what people value versus what is good for them... find ways to monetize what people value in order to give them what they really need."
