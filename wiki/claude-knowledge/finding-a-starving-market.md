## The starving crowd concept

From a marketing professor's classroom example Hormozi retells: asked what single advantage they'd want for a hot dog stand (location, quality, low prices, best taste), students eventually run out of answers — the professor's actual answer is "a starving crowd." A stand with the worst hot dogs, worst prices, and worst location will still sell out if it's the only stand available to a genuinely starving, sufficiently large crowd (his example: after a college football game). Extreme real-world case: toilet paper during the COVID-19 shortage — no compelling offer, no sales pitch, terrible pricing, and rolls sold for $100+ purely because demand was so large relative to supply.

**The framing this produces**: with enough market demand, you can be mediocre at business, have a bad offer, and be bad at persuasion, and still make money. Market strength is the highest-order lever — it can compensate for weakness everywhere else. This grounds the "order of importance" hierarchy below.

## Cautionary tale: Lloyd, newspapers, and masks

A friend of Hormozi's ran a software business for almost a decade, selling newspapers self-serve digital ad tools with a pure revenue-share pricing model (zero cost to the newspaper if it didn't work — "pure gain for the papers"). Great product, zero-risk offer, and Lloyd himself was "a natural salesman." The business still declined steadily. Hormozi's diagnosis, which he says was obvious to him but not to Lloyd (a smarter operator than himself, in his telling): **the newspaper market itself was shrinking ~25%/year.** Lloyd tried every angle — product, offer, sales skill — except questioning the market, because entrepreneurs "hate quitting" and are trained to keep solving problems rather than reconsidering whether the problem is solvable at all. He eventually downsized.

When COVID hit, Lloyd pivoted to automated mask manufacturing, using new technology to beat Chinese import costs per mask, and was doing millions per month within five months — same entrepreneur, same underlying skill set, radically different result, purely from switching markets. Hormozi's explicit takeaway: **"everyone is affected by their market"** — this book's entire framework assumes at minimum a normal market (growing at the marketplace's average rate, with unmet needs falling into health, wealth, or relationships); nothing in the book would have worked for Lloyd while he was targeting newspapers specifically.

## The four market indicators

When picking a market, Hormozi looks for:

1. **Massive pain** — the market must *desperately need*, not just want, the solution. Pain can be almost anything (being broke, a bad marriage, waiting in line, back pain, feeling unattractive, being overweight). His training maxim for salespeople: **"the pain is the pitch"** — accurately articulating a prospect's pain reliably gets them to buy the solution. Pro tip he gives alongside this: "the point of good writing is for the reader to understand; the point of good persuasion is for the prospect to *feel* understood." The degree of pain is directly proportional to the price a market will bear (ties into the Value Equation's dream-outcome driver).
2. **Purchasing power** — the market must actually be able to afford your price. His illustrative failure case: a friend built a genuinely excellent resume-improvement service for job seekers — high pain, large and growing pool, easy to target — but couldn't get anyone to pay, because the entire audience was unemployed and therefore broke. Massive pain without purchasing power still fails.
3. **Easy to target** — a market you can't actually reach is unusable regardless of how well-suited your offer is. Practical markers: does the audience have associations, mailing lists, social media groups, or channels they're already gathered in? Hormozi is explicit this is a tactical/logistical point, not theoretical — e.g., wanting to serve "rich doctors" is fine as a target in principle, but the ad platform's actual delivery has to reach them, not adjacent groups like nursing students, or the offer (however good) never gets seen by the right eyes.
4. **Growing** — a growing market is "a tailwind," a shrinking one "a headwind" that fights every effort. This was Lloyd's exact failure mode: newspapers scored well on pain, purchasing power, and targetability, but were shrinking fast enough to sink the business anyway.

**The three markets that always exist, per Hormozi**: health, wealth, and relationships — because pain in their absence never disappears. The exercise he recommends: find a *narrower subgroup* within one of these three broad buckets that scores well on pain, purchasing power, and targeting, and isn't shrinking. His own example: rather than "relationship coaching" broadly, he'd target second-half-of-life relationship coaching for older adults over college-student relationship coaching — seniors living alone are (his claim) in more pain nearing end of life, have more purchasing power, and are easier to target; he also notes that at time of writing, more people were turning 65/year than turning 20 (a growing subgroup).

## Order of importance: market > offer > persuasion skill

Hormozi's stated hierarchy, rated informally as great/normal/bad at each level:

> Starving crowd (market) > offer strength > persuasion skill

- A **great** market overpowers weakness everywhere below it — bad offer, bad persuasion, still profitable (the hot dog stand at 2am outside a bar).
- A **normal** market plus a **grand slam offer** is highly profitable even with weak persuasion skills — Hormozi states this is the position "most people reading this book" are actually in, and the explicit reason he wrote the book.
- A **normal** market with a **normal** offer requires *exceptional* persuasion skill to succeed — the hardest, most effort-intensive path, though he notes many empires have been built this way. Nailing the offer is presented as the shortcut around needing that level of persuasion mastery.
- A **bad** market caps out the whole chain regardless of offer or skill quality, unless a great rating higher in the chain compensates for it.

## Commit to the niche ("niche slap")

Hormozi's coined term for a specific failure pattern: a newer entrepreneur half-heartedly tries one offer in one market, doesn't hit a million dollars, and concludes the *market* was bad — when in reality they simply haven't found a grand slam offer for that market yet. His example: switching from "helping dentists" to "helping chiropractors" when both are enormous, viable markets — the fix isn't switching markets, it's committing and iterating within one. His framing: "all businesses and all markets have unpleasant characteristics — the grass is never greener." Repeated niche-hopping means restarting the trial-and-error clock from zero every time, which guarantees longer failure, not shorter. **"You will fail until you succeed"** — but only if you stay in the arena long enough to actually iterate.

## Niching down and the Dan Kennedy pricing example

Attribution: Hormozi credits Dan Kennedy directly for this concept, calling it life-changing. **For most people under $10M/year, niching down makes significantly more money for an almost identical product** — Hormozi's caveat is that above $10M/year, further niching starts to bump against your market's actual TAM (total addressable market), a business can only grow to meet its TAM, and only ~0.4% (1-in-250) of businesses ever reach $10M/year in the first place, so this ceiling is a late-stage problem for the overwhelming majority of readers.

Worked example (a generic "time management" course, same underlying content at every step):

| Positioning | Price |
| --- | --- |
| Time management (generic) | $19 |
| Time management for sales professionals | $99 |
| Time management for outbound B2B sales | $499 |
| Time management for outbound B2B power tools & gardening sales reps | $1,997 |

That's a ~100x price increase for what is essentially the same core content, because at each step of specificity the buyer's belief that "this is made exactly for me" grows, and the messaging can speak directly to what a single deal or sale is actually worth to that exact reader (e.g., a B2B rep whose average deal nets $500+ can easily justify a $499 purchase). Hormozi draws the same comparison for fitness content: a generic weight-loss program might sell for $19, while a program marketed specifically to shift-working nurses — same core "eat less, move more" — might sell for $1,997.

Hormozi's own applied example: he moved from generic small-business acquisition consulting to a hyper-specific avatar — a micro gym owner, ~100 members, signed lease, at least one employee, wanting to help clients lose weight — and turned away anyone outside that exact avatar (personal trainers, online coaches) even though he could have helped them, because the narrow focus sharpened messaging and conversion. His own portfolio companies are majority *non*-gym businesses, underscoring that the niching choice was a go-to-market decision, not a capability limit.

**Practical framing he offers for anyone who feels capped**: if you're at $1M–$3M/year and think you've hit a ceiling requiring you to broaden, "you are wrong — you just need to be better" within the niche, before assuming expansion is the answer.
