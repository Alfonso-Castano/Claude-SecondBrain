## Divergent vs. convergent thinking (the mental mode this whole process requires)

Hormozi opens with a distinction he says is essential to actually doing this exercise well. **Convergent problem-solving**: many known variables, unchanging conditions, one correct answer — math-class thinking (his example: given call volume, close rate, and a sales target, calculate exactly how many reps to hire). Schools train this because it's easy to grade, but it's the wrong mode for offer-building. **Divergent thinking**: many variables (known and unknown), dynamic conditions, and *multiple* valid answers, some better than others — this is the mode real offer creativity requires, and it's not taught or rewarded the way convergent thinking is.

**The brick exercise** (his own warm-up drill, meant to be done literally, in ~120 seconds): write down as many different uses for a brick as possible. His own list, as an example of range: paperweight, doorstop, building material, fish-tank decoration, planter, trophy, mural piece (painted small), resistance-training weight, wedge, pen holder, kids' toy, flotation device (if plastic), payment/store-of-value (if gold), flagpole holder, seat (jumbo brick). The exercise question that unlocks more answers: does the brick's size, material, or shape have to stay fixed? Once you let those vary, the list grows substantially. The transferable lesson: every offer has interchangeable "building blocks," and the goal is applying this same divergent stretching to how many ways you could combine them to create value for a specific customer.

## Step 1: Identify the dream outcome

Hormozi's own founding example (his gym): he couldn't sell $99/month boot camp memberships against $29/month competitors like LA Fitness, and couldn't even give the service away for free, because people didn't want a "membership" at all — they wanted to lose weight. His reframe: "I wasn't selling my membership anymore. I wasn't selling the plane flight. I was selling the vacation." Concretely, this meant repositioning from "gym membership" to "lose 20 lbs in 6 weeks" — a specific, time-bound dream outcome rather than a generic access product.

## Step 2: List every problem in the way

List everything that could stop the prospect from reaching the dream outcome, thought through in the literal sequence they'd encounter it (Hormozi's instruction: "channel insane detail" — the more problems surfaced, the more valuable the eventual offer, since every problem becomes an opportunity to add value). His worked example (weight loss), broken into the customer's actual sequence of actions — buying healthy food → cooking it → eating it → exercising — with each step's problems mapped onto the four Value Equation drivers as a generating structure:

1. **Dream outcome friction**: "this won't be financially worth it."
2. **Likelihood-of-achievement friction**: "it won't work for me specifically," "I won't stick with it," "external factors (family, travel) will get in the way" — he flags this bucket as the most business-specific and least generic of the four.
3. **Effort/sacrifice friction**: "this will be too hard/confusing, I'll suck at it, I won't like it."
4. **Time friction**: "this takes too long," "I'm too busy," "it's inconvenient."

Running this systematically across even a handful of core actions (his example: 4 core actions × roughly 4 sub-problems each) produces on the order of 16 core problems and 32–64 total sub-problems — which he frames as reassuring rather than overwhelming: "the more problems you think of, the more problems you get to solve."

**A second worked example, from a different niche entirely, to show the process isn't fitness-specific**: Hormozi cites Brooke Castillo (a life-coaching business owner) running this same problem/solution exercise for a 90-day relationship course. Her dream outcome: "amazing, loving relationship in 90 days." Her problem list: no good options, not attractive, not available, boring, no chemistry, poor communication, sex isn't good, no intellectual stimulation, not enough effort, no time, insecurity, unmet needs/expectations, emotional volatility, relationship feels dull, partners want different things, moving too slow or fizzling out fast, kids complicating things, sexual incompatibility. Her corresponding solutions list (same problem→solution reversal mechanic): how to get a list of prospective partners to invite into the 90 days, how to be attracted to your chosen partner, how to find an available partner, how to keep 90 days exciting and never boring, how to create real chemistry, how to communicate in sexy/fun/meaningful ways, how to make the relationship hot by being hot, how to have great sex for 90 days, how to create intellectual stimulation, how to put in effort for maximum return, how to create hourly "dopamine/love hits," how to overcome insecurity within the 90 days. Hormozi's stated takeaway from including it: "don't be fancy — just get all the problems down, then turn them into solutions," regardless of whether the underlying business is fitness, relationships, or something else entirely.

## Step 3: Turn every problem into a named solution

Mechanical, two-part step: (1) reverse each problem statement into solution-oriented language ("it's hard and confusing" → "how to make it easy and enjoyable"), (2) give the resulting solution a name. Hormozi frames this explicitly as "copywriting 101," out of scope to teach in depth here, but functionally it produces a literal checklist of everything the offer will need to solve. He is explicit and repeats it for emphasis: **you will solve every problem on the list** — this is a constraint, not optional coverage.

Worked example from his weight-loss business (abbreviated — problem → solution pairing): "buying healthy food is hard/confusing" → "how to make buying healthy food easy and enjoyable, especially for busy moms"; "takes too much time" → "how to buy healthy food quickly"; "it's expensive" → "how to buy healthy food for less than your current grocery bill"; "unsustainable long-term" → "how to make buying healthy food take less effort than buying unhealthy food"; "family needs conflict" → "how to buy for you and your family at once"; "travel disrupts it" → "how to get healthy food while traveling" — and the same pattern repeated for cooking, eating, and exercising.

**Anecdote on why solving every problem matters, not just most of them**: early in his weight-loss business, Hormozi refused to work with clients who ate out for lunch regularly, insisting on 100% home-prepared meals, and lost sales over it. Under financial pressure, he finally conceded to one prospect (a business exec) by improvising an "eating out guide" on the spot — closing the sale. From that point on, whenever "what about eating out?" came up, he had a ready answer, and kept adding similar one-off fixes over time until there were no remaining single objections that could kill a sale. His stated lesson: **"don't get romantic about how you want to solve the problem — find a way to solve every problem a prospect presents with."** Leaving even one unresolved objection standing measurably costs sales, even if it doesn't cost every sale.

## Step 4: Brainstorm delivery vehicles for every solution ("the how")

For each named solution, divergently brainstorm every possible way it could actually be delivered — again pushing past the obvious default, including options you wouldn't actually commit to, purely to jog different variations into view. Hormozi frames this as one-time, high-leverage work: "you only need to do this once... for a product that may last years... you get paid for thinking."

**Organizing structure — group by audience scale.** Using his grocery-shopping example:

- *One-on-one*: in-person grocery shopping with the client, a personalized grocery list built with them, full-service shopping done entirely for them, in-person (off-site) orientation, text support while they shop, a live phone call while they shop.
- *Small group*: group in-person shopping trips, group list-building sessions (one-time or recurring weekly), buying and delivering groceries for the whole group, off-site group orientation.
- *One-to-many*: a live-streamed virtual grocery tour with Q&A, a pre-recorded grocery tour used as a standing reference asset, a DIY shareable grocery calculator tool, pre-built weekly shopping lists bundled with the plan, a "grocery buddy" pairing system, pre-made Instacart carts for one-click delivery.

**"Cheat codes" for generating more variation**, given as a reusable checklist:

1. Personal-attention level: one-on-one, small group, or one-to-many.
2. Effort expected from the client: DIY (do it themselves), DWY (do it *with* them — you teach), or DFY (done *for* them).
3. If live, delivery medium: in person, phone, email, text, Zoom, or chat.
4. If recorded, consumption format: audio, video, or written.
5. Response-time commitment: 24/7, 9–5, within 5 minutes, within an hour, within 24 hours.
6. **The 10x / 1/10th test**: if the customer paid 10x your price (or $100,000), what would you provide? If they paid 1/10th your price and you *still* had to make it more valuable than it already is, how would you deliver that? Stretching in both directions surfaces solutions you wouldn't otherwise consider.

## Step 5a: Trim — keep only high-value, appropriate-cost items

From the resulting giant list, remove items that are high-cost-to-you and low-value-to-them first, then remove low-cost/low-value items. To judge value, Hormozi's filter is again the Value Equation: does this item make the prospect (1) financially value it, (2) believe they're more likely to succeed, (3) feel less effort/sacrifice is required, (4) get the result faster? What survives should be either **low-cost-high-value** or **high-cost-high-value** — nothing low-value should remain regardless of its cost to deliver.

**On things too expensive to offer as-is**: if an ideal-but-uneconomical version exists (his example: moving in with a client to personally handle their shopping, cooking, and exercise — something he says he'd only do "for a gazillion dollars"), the move isn't to discard the idea but to step back one increment at a time until you find a version whose cost you're willing to absorb at your actual price point — or alternatively, raise price enough that the original version becomes viable.

**Bias toward high-value, one-to-many delivery vehicles**, because they typically have the widest gap between what they cost you to build and the value they deliver. His example: before his gym business, he built a small Excel tool that generated over 100 personalized meal plans from a client's stated goals, including exact grocery quantities and prep instructions — about 100 hours of one-time build effort, after which each personalized plan took roughly 15 minutes to generate and sold for a high price. **This is explicitly why software becomes so valuable** — high one-time creation cost, near-zero marginal cost thereafter. He notes this doesn't mean never using one-on-one delivery — he still does one-on-one work himself with his portfolio companies' COOs — but that scarce, expensive delivery modes should be reserved specifically for the highest-value offer components, not spread across everything.

**Concrete asset payoff example**: meal plans he built once for his own gym have since been reused across 4,000+ licensed gyms and hundreds of thousands of end clients — a week or two of upfront work amortized indefinitely. He draws the same parallel to this very book: expensive to write once, cheap to distribute to every subsequent reader.

## Step 5b: Stack — assemble the trimmed items into named bundles

The final step packages the surviving high-value items into named, individually-valued bundles, each addressing one category of problem, then sums them into a headline total-value-vs-price comparison. Worked example from the weight-loss offer (abbreviated to the bundle names, contents summary, and stated value):

- **"Foolproof Bargain Grocery System"** ($1,000 value) — nutrition orientation, recorded grocery tour, DIY grocery calculator, weekly lists, bargain-shopping training, grocery buddy system, pre-made Instacart carts, weekly text check-in.
- **"Ready in 5 Minutes" busy-parent cooking guide** ($600 value) — orientation, meal-prep instructions, DIY calculator, weekly meal-prep instructions, meal-prep buddy system, under-5-minutes healthy snacks guide, weekly accountability post.
- **Personalized "lick your fingers good" meal plan** ($500 value) — orientation, personalized meal plan, 5-minute shake/lunch/dinner guides, family-sized meal versions, daily meal photo check-in, one-on-one adjustment/upsell meeting.
- **Fat-burning workout system** ($699 value) — adjusted to the individual so they never overtrain, plateau, or get injured.
- **Travel workout/eating blueprint** ($199 value) — no-equipment workouts for travel.
- **"Never Fall Off" accountability system** ($1,000 value) — described as working "without your permission," credited with getting people who dislike the gym to look forward to attending.
- **"Live It Up While Slimming Down" eating-out system** ($349 value) — lets clients eat out and stay social without falling off the plan.

**Total stated value: $4,351 — sold for $599.** Hormozi's author's note: as the business improved at creating and communicating this kind of value, the actual facilities selling this same style of bundle later charged $2,400–$5,200 for comparable programs over longer terms — the value-stacking skill compounds over time, with each new value-add layering onto everything built before it.

He names three things a properly stacked bundle accomplishes: (1) it solves *all* the perceived problems, not just some; (2) it gives the prospect genuine conviction the offer is one-of-a-kind; (3) it makes direct price comparison to competitors effectively impossible.

## The sales-to-fulfillment continuum

A framing tool for how much to build into the offer at the outset: there's a tradeoff between ease-of-fulfillment and ease-of-sale — doing less makes something harder to sell, doing more makes it easier to sell but harder to fulfill. Hormozi's stated mantra for sequencing this tradeoff: **"create flow, monetize flow, then add friction."** Generate demand first, get people to say yes with an offer that may initially over-deliver relative to what's sustainable, and only *then*, once cash flow and real customer feedback exist, start trimming or systematizing to make fulfillment sustainable. His stated reasoning: it's better to have cash flow and real signal about what's working, even at a temporary fulfillment cost, than to have a perfectly efficient offer with zero customers and no data.

**Illustrative case — how his own delivery model evolved under this principle**: initially, Hormozi personally flew to each gym, covered his own travel costs, generated and worked the leads himself, and even ran the first onboarding meeting — taking on all financial and time risk himself while charging only for results (gym owners put down a fully refundable $500 to reserve a date). This was maximally sales-friendly (zero risk to the client) but eventually became unsustainable to fulfill even with a team of 8 people selling. The fix wasn't changing the underlying promise ("I will fill your gym in 30 days" stayed the same) — it was changing *how* it was delivered: teaching gym owners to run the same process themselves rather than doing it personally, which let him serve hundreds of gyms a month instead of 8, at roughly a third of the price per gym, without traveling at all.
