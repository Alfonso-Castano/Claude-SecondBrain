## Framing: the delicate dance of desire

Hormozi's premise: all marketing is ultimately an attempt to shift the supply-and-demand curve — increasing demand through persuasive communication, and/or decreasing (perceived) supply, and the ideal combination is maximum demand against minimum supply. He cites Naval Ravikant: **"desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want"** — meaning we only want what we don't yet have, and desire for something evaporates once it's obtained. The direct implication he draws: to *sustain* demand, a business must deliberately not fully satisfy it — selling fewer units than it's capable of selling.

**Illustrative scenario contrast**: selling 10 slots of a $500 workshop to everyone who wants one (all demand satisfied) vs. selling only 2 slots at $5,000 each out of a pool that could have supported 10 — leaving 8 people with unmet desire. Hormozi's claim, framed as roughly an 80/20 pattern he's observed rather than a rule: demand for services tends to be non-linear/fractal, with roughly a fifth of prospects willing to pay 5x+ the price of the rest. Selling the scarcer, pricier version to that subgroup produces more revenue, lower fulfillment cost, and — critically — the 8 people who *didn't* get in leave with amplified, not diminished, desire, plus a compounding effect: their unmet interest attracts new prospects (via social proof / observing others' enthusiasm), so the next time the offer opens, even more demand exists than before, provided the door doesn't open too wide too soon.

**"Hormozi's law," as he names it himself**: "the longer you delay the ask, the bigger the ask you can make — the longer the runway, the bigger the plane that can take off." The core discipline: keep supply/satisfaction of desire deliberately under the demand you're capable of generating, in both directions — satisfying zero demand (never selling) starves the business and alienates prospects over time, while satisfying *all* demand ("if you sleep with your significant other every day, they have less desire than if you haven't for a week," his own analogy) kills the "golden goose." The craft is calibrating between those two failure modes, not eliminating scarcity/urgency altogether.

This section covers the five outside-the-core-offer levers Hormozi uses to manage that curve: scarcity, urgency, bonuses, guarantees, and (on a companion page) naming.

## Scarcity

**Mechanism**: fixed or explicitly limited supply/quantity, which triggers fear of missing out and increases urgency to act. Grounded in loss aversion — Hormozi's framing: "fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain," so scarcity is a more reliable behavioral lever than most positive-appeal messaging alone.

**Three types he names**: (1) limited supply of seats/slots overall or within a period, (2) limited supply of bonuses, (3) "never available again."

**Illustrative real-world case — Arnold Schwarzenegger charity fundraiser**: Hormozi recounts attending Arnold's After School All Stars fundraiser, where a major donor named George advised the charity's CEO to *cut* the number of tickets sold and *raise* the price (from $15,000 to $25,000/ticket) specifically because demand was already high — "when demand increases, cut supply." The event raised nearly $5.4M from just 100 attendees ($54,000/head), auctioning one-of-a-kind items (each explicitly framed as unrepeatable if missed) with Arnold occasionally letting two competing high bidders both "win" to capture both donations. Hormozi's stated takeaway: the underlying products/experiences didn't change — an item that might not sell for $10,000 elsewhere sold for $100,000 in that scarcity-and-status-engineered setting.

**Physical product tactics**: limited flavor/color/size releases (his example: "this month we're releasing 100 boxes of mint chocolate cookie protein bars"). Two operating rules he's explicit about: (1) **you should always actually sell out** — better to under-order and reliably sell out than over-order and fail to create genuine scarcity, and this compounds in effectiveness the more consistently it's done (he cites roughly once a month as a sweet spot for businesses that use this repeatedly); (2) you must **publicize that you sold out** — this converts fence-sitters via social proof (evidence others valued it) and increases their urgency the next time a batch opens, since the choice to buy has now effectively been made for them by circumstance. Named example: Chanel, which deliberately sends only 1–2 units of each piece to each store, making every item effectively "the last or second-to-last in stock" everywhere, sustaining premium pricing for over a century.

**Service-business tactics** (three variants, same underlying mechanism):

1. **Total business cap** — a hard ceiling on total active clients at a tier ("my agency only services 25 customers, period"), with a waitlist for the rest; periodically raising the cap by 10–20% then re-capping, or squeezing out low performers to make room for higher-value replacements over time.
2. **Growth-rate cap** — a cap on new clients per period ("we only accept 5 new clients a week; 3 spots are already taken this week"). Hormozi notes he's used this since the start of his career, simply because *every* business has some real weekly capacity ceiling anyway — this tactic is largely just being transparent about a constraint that already exists rather than inventing an artificial one.
3. **Cohort cap** — a fixed number of clients accepted per cohort/class on a set cadence (monthly, quarterly), which doubles as an operational onboarding structure.

**Extending scarcity to free lead magnets**: capping a free download to, say, 20 people/week, with a waitlist notification when the next batch opens, converts an ordinary free asset into something people actively chase — and, Hormozi notes, people also consume things more attentively once they've had to wait or compete for access.

**"Honest scarcity" — his stated most ethical version**: simply state your real current capacity truthfully (e.g., "we're at 81% of our total capacity this week") rather than fabricating a number. This works because it implicitly communicates social proof (a large share of your stated capacity is already claimed) while requiring no invented constraint — the business owner just has to actually decide, and hold to, where the real ceiling is.

**Two additional named tactics**: (a) reserving genuinely exclusive one-on-one access channels (DM, personal email, phone, voice memo, Zoom) at a very high price and very low headcount — Hormozi's framing: "if you don't hate money, sell a very limited supply of one-on-one access" and pick the access channel that suits your own working style (he personally dislikes email/DM but doesn't mind calls); (b) "once you're out, you can never come back" — capping a premium tier and stating that leaving it forfeits it permanently, which he's used at multiple levels of his gym businesses; he notes this works best at smaller group sizes and loses potency as group size grows.

## Urgency

**Mechanism**: scarcity limits *quantity*; urgency limits *time* — a defined deadline or cutoff for a decision. Hormozi's four named tactics:

1. **Cohort-based rolling urgency** — clients start on a fixed cadence (e.g., weekly), so signing up today means starting Monday, waiting means starting a week later. He describes a stronger variant: mentioning a recent dropout freed an opening ("I actually had a client drop out, so I have a spot in Monday's cohort"). He states these small framings have pushed many otherwise-undecided prospects over the edge, purely by making the cost of delay concrete (a full week lost) rather than abstract. He also addresses the fear of losing sales by delaying start: citing that the last ~4 hours of a week-long launch campaign typically produce 50–60% of total sales (i.e., the final ~3% of available time produces the majority of results) — evidence, in his reading, that most "lost" late deciders were never going to buy regardless, so genuine urgency isn't sacrificing real sales, just accelerating decisions that were already being made.
2. **Rolling seasonal urgency** — real, visible promotion end-dates in copy/landing pages, refreshed regularly under new seasonal names (his example sequence: "New Year Promotion" → "Valentine's Lovers Promo" → "Sexy by Spring" → "Fools in Love April Promo") — same underlying offer each time, just re-wrapped and re-dated, which he says takes roughly 5 minutes to refresh and produces a real conversion lift. He explicitly flags his #1 local-business strategy is this variant: local advertisers need to vary messaging more frequently than national ones, and a fresh date/name on the same core service reliably outperforms the same unchanged campaign run on repeat.
3. **Pricing/bonus-based urgency** — attaching the urgency to a promotional price, discount, or bonus rather than to service availability itself, so a business that serves clients year-round can still create real urgency ("not sure how long we'll run this discount — we change it every ~4 weeks"). He notes this preserves integrity for businesses that will, in fact, serve a client whenever they buy — the deadline is real, but it's a deadline on the *deal*, not on access to the underlying service. Related pro tip: **always announce price increases before raising prices** — it creates a natural pipeline-cleaning event, gives a legitimate urgency hook, and "shows a position of strength."
4. **Exploding opportunity** — situations with a genuinely time-decaying opportunity independent of the seller's own artificial framing (his examples: arbitrage between marketplaces, early entry into a new advertising platform before competitors saturate it, job offers whose compensation decreases the longer a candidate delays accepting). The urgency here is inherent to the opportunity, not manufactured.

## Bonuses

**Core mechanism**: a single bundled offer is *less* persuasive than the same content broken into named, individually-valued component parts and stacked one at a time — this is why "but wait, there's more" infomercial structures work, and Hormozi credits Jason Fladlien for his renewed appreciation of the technique. Presenting one knife for $38.95, then progressively adding 37 more knives, sharpeners, pans, and guarantees, widens the price-to-value discrepancy with each addition rather than lowering price — "we anchor the price... then with each increasingly valuable bonus, that discrepancy grows wider... until we snap the rubber band... holding their wallet in their pocket."

**Explicit rule: add bonuses instead of discounting.** Discounting the core offer to close a deal teaches a customer that price is negotiable — a durable, compounding problem for every future transaction. Adding value (bonuses) instead preserves price integrity while achieving the same psychological effect of "getting a deal."

**Sequencing in one-on-one selling**: ask for the sale *before* revealing bonuses. If the prospect says yes, bonuses are then revealed as a reinforcing surprise ("wow" moment) after commitment. If they say no, a bonus specifically matched to their stated objection is offered before asking again — Hormozi notes reciprocity makes it psychologically hard for people to keep declining as bonuses accumulate to address each stated concern.

**Eleven rules for presenting a bonus, given as a checklist**: (1) always present bonuses, using the bundle built in the core-offer construction process; (2) give each a specific benefit-forward name; (3) explain how it relates to the prospect's specific problem, what it is, how/why it was created (adds credibility), and how it will specifically improve their outcome (tie back to the Value Equation); (4) provide proof it works (a stat, past client, or personal result); (5) paint a vivid picture of them already experiencing its benefit; (6) always attach and justify a specific price tag; (7) prefer tools/checklists over additional trainings, since lower delivery effort with equivalent perceived value raises the value-per-cost ratio; (8) each bonus should address a *specific* stated fear/obstacle and disprove it; (9) alternatively, a bonus can preemptively solve the *next* problem the customer will logically hit before they even encounter it; (10) the cumulative value of the bonuses should exceed the value of the core offer itself — psychologically this also implies the core offer must be even more valuable than an already-impressive bonus stack; (11) bonuses can themselves carry scarcity ("only buyers of X get bonuses 1–3, never sold separately elsewhere") or urgency ("bonus X, normally $1,000, is included only if you buy today") layered on top for compounding effect.

**Advanced tactic — other businesses' products/services as bonuses, at no direct cost**: negotiate with complementary (non-competing) businesses to provide free services/products as bonuses in exchange for exposure to your customer base. Worked example (a hypothetical pain clinic): free massages from a massage therapist, free adjustments from a chiropractor, discounts from a low-inflammation food company, discounted braces/orthotics, a free personal-training session and pool membership month from a local health club, pharmacy discounts — stacked, these bonuses alone can exceed the core offer's price. Taken further, Hormozi describes negotiating **referral commissions** on top of the free bonus exposure — his real example: his gym-owner clients' athletes get a 30% discount from his supplement company, Prestige Labs, and the referring athlete earns 40% of resulting sales after the discount — a three-way win (client savings, athlete income, new customer acquisition). A fully worked numeric example: a $400 core offer where negotiated referral commissions from partner businesses (chiropractor $100/referral, food company free product, orthotics $100/referral, health club $50/signup or free membership, pharmacy $100/referral) generate an additional $350 in profit per sale, entirely from referring already-acquired customers elsewhere — no additional acquisition cost.

**What belongs in a bonus vs. the core offer**: short answer, "wow factor" — content that's high-value but compact enough to stand alone and would otherwise get buried inside a larger bundle. Checklists and infographics are cited as especially good bonus material because they condense significant value into something someone might not pay much for as a standalone product but perceives as very valuable when framed as an add-on.

## Guarantees

**Framing**: risk is "the single greatest objection for any product or service" — reversing it is one of the highest-leverage things an offer can do. Hormozi cites Jason Fladlien's claim of seeing 2–4x conversion differences purely from changing the *quality* of a guarantee, and advises spending disproportionate time crafting the guarantee relative to its apparent simplicity.

**The math on why strong guarantees still win even with more refunds**: worked example — 100 sales at a 5% refund rate nets 95 sales; a stronger guarantee that grows sales to 130 at a doubled 10% refund rate nets 117 sales — still a 23% net increase (117/95 = 1.23x). Hormozi's rule of thumb: for a guarantee to *not* be worth adding, the absolute increase in refund rate would need to fully offset the absolute increase in sales rate — which would typically require refunds to more than double, an unlikely outcome in practice. His explicit caution alongside this: buyers who purchase *specifically because of* the guarantee, rather than because of the product, can be lower-quality customers less inclined to put in the work needed to succeed — tying guarantee conditions to the actions a customer actually needs to take can filter for and produce better outcomes for both sides.

**Four types of guarantee, in increasing order of the seller's own required underlying confidence:**

1. **Unconditional** — the strongest form; essentially "buy first, then judge," no conditions attached (e.g., "20 clients in 30 days or your money back, no questions asked"). Highest conversion, but also the highest refund exposure — best suited to lower-cost-to-fulfill, lower-ticket, B2C-leaning offers. Hormozi's own historical example: an unconditional satisfaction guarantee on his weight-loss program ("if at any time you're not satisfied, you can request a refund at any time") — of roughly 4,000 sales over 3.5 years, only 2 people ever invoked it.
2. **Conditional** — the guarantee only holds if the customer meets specific stated conditions (showing up, following the process, hitting checkpoints). Hormozi's stated preference: conditional guarantees, well-designed, can be *stronger* persuasion tools than unconditional ones (since conditions imply real confidence and match the buyer's investment level to expectations) while also functioning as a genuine mechanism for improving actual client outcomes, since the conditions can literally be the actions that drive success. He notes that, given a real choice between a refund and actually getting the promised outcome, most people choose the outcome — meaning a well-designed conditional guarantee's real function is often to keep pushing toward delivering the result, not to process refunds.
3. **Anti-guarantee** — "all sales are final," explicitly and deliberately, typically justified by real exposure/vulnerability in what's being sold (his example: code, scripts, or messaging templates that, once seen, could be copied without payment). He frames this as an intentional strength signal rather than an absence of a guarantee — "if you're the type of customer who needs a guarantee before taking the jump, you are not the type of person we want to work with."
4. **Implied guarantees** — performance-based structures (rev-share, profit-share, pay-per-result, ratchets/tiers, bonus triggers) where payment is contingent on the seller actually producing results. Hormozi calls this "one of, if not the most desirable" structure of all, since it fully aligns incentives, self-selects for low performers to leave (they simply don't get paid), and fosters genuine collaboration — with the tradeoff being tracking/collection complexity. He notes it can be combined with a minimum floor (e.g., "the greater of $1,000 or 10% of revenue generated") to cover baseline fulfillment cost while still preserving upside alignment.

**Guarantees can be stacked** (analogous to bonuses): e.g., an unconditional 30-day no-questions refund *plus* a conditional "triple your money back in 90 days if you do X, Y, Z" — or two sequential conditional guarantees tied to a timeline ("$10,000 by day 60, $30,000 by day 90, provided you do 1/2/3"), which future-paces the prospect into believing the outcome is a near-certainty since it's spelled out with specific milestones.

**Named, specific guarantee variants he catalogs** (each with a stated best-fit use case): full/partial refund, refund of ad spend/ancillary costs, "we pay for a competitor's program instead," refund plus a stated cash bonus; **service guarantee** ("we keep working for free until X is achieved," which he calls "probably my personal favorite... it essentially guarantees they will achieve their goal" by eliminating the time dimension — he reports that in practice almost no client who is genuinely following the process ever needs to invoke it, since results usually arrive or the guarantee quietly lapses as the relationship continues past its original terms); **modified service guarantee** (extended access/service for a defined additional period, generally at least 2x the original term, rather than open-ended); **credit-based guarantee** (refund issued as credit toward other services — useful specifically for upsells); **personal service guarantee** ("I personally work with you free until X is achieved" — he calls this extremely powerful but explicitly warns it needs tight conditions attached, e.g., prior spend thresholds and required responsiveness, or it becomes operationally unmanageable); **hotel/airfare-cost guarantee** (reimbursing ancillary travel costs, not just the product price — used well for in-person events/workshops, and often larger in dollar terms than the underlying registration fee); **wage-payment guarantee** (reimbursing the client's own hourly rate if a session isn't valuable — he notes this sounds risky but is essentially never claimed in practice since almost no one calculates and requests their actual imputed hourly wage); **release-of-contract guarantee** (letting a client out of a contract with no penalty — costless to offer if you don't actually enforce contract penalties anyway).

**Naming/wording guarantees**: Hormozi advises giving the guarantee a distinctive, memorable name rather than a generic label like "30-day money-back satisfaction guarantee" — his examples: "the shark-infested waters guarantee" ("if you wouldn't jump into shark-infested waters to get our product back, we'll return every dollar"), or "the club a baby seal guarantee." He also reproduces, verbatim and credited, Jason Fladlien's full unconditional-guarantee webinar wording as a model of how to frame a strong guarantee persuasively without sounding desperate (paraphrased core structure: "I'm not asking you to decide yes or no today — I'm asking you to make a fully informed decision, which you can only make from the inside... if you aren't happy, I'm not happy, for any reason whatsoever... just ask and you'll get it").

**Cross-cutting rule for building your own guarantee**: identify the customer's biggest specific fear about what happens if they pay you and don't get the result, then reverse *that exact fear* directly into the guarantee's structure — generic guarantees are weaker than ones clearly built to neutralize a named, specific objection. Hormozi's closing caveat: a guarantee is an *enhancer*, not a substitute for a real product or sales process — used to paper over a weak offer or weak sales team, it will simply generate a flood of refunds rather than fixing the underlying problem.
