## Why naming matters as its own lever

Hormozi's framing: a grand slam offer only makes money once prospects actually notice it and are moved to act — naming determines response rate to ads, outbound (cold email/call/text), and inbound organic engagement, independent of how good the underlying offer actually is. His illustrative example: a "free six week stress release challenge" and a "float tank center session" can describe the literal same underlying thing, but the first name reliably gets a much stronger response — the wrapping paper changes behavior even when the contents don't.

**The strategic purpose of renaming: fighting offer fatigue without changing the offer itself.** Over time, and especially in local markets (where a limited addressable population sees the same ad repeatedly at low cost), offers fatigue — audiences stop responding to a message they've now seen many times. Hormozi's explicit disclaimer: seeing an offer *once* does not fatigue it — most people don't even register an offer on first exposure, so ordinary creative refreshes (new images, new copy) can sustain a live offer for a long time. Genuine fatigue is a phenomenon of years of repeated exposure, not weeks. The core reframe naming enables: **you are never changing the actual offer (the work, the deliverables, the price) — only the "wrapping paper" (the name/positioning) that presents it to the market.**

## The MAGIC formula

Hormozi's naming framework, spelled out component by component (not all five are mandatory — he recommends using three to five of them per name, since fitting all five often makes a name too long; brevity vs. specificity is a real tradeoff to test for):

- **M — Magnetic reason why.** A word or phrase stating why this promotion exists right now, or why the prospect should respond. Hormozi's framing device: think like a fraternity party planner — the stated reason can be almost anything, even absurd, as long as it's stated with conviction (his own college anecdote: a party thrown because a fraternity brother got his wisdom teeth removed). It should implicitly answer "why are they running this?" or "what's in it for me?" Examples given: free, 88% off, giveaway, spring/summer/back-to-school, grand opening, new management, anniversary, Halloween, new year.
- **A — Announce your avatar.** Name the specific ideal customer being targeted — both signaling who it's for and, implicitly, who it's *not* for. For local businesses specifically, Hormozi advises going as hyper-local as possible rather than city-level (his example: not "Baltimore," but "Towson, Maryland") — more specific local framing converts better. Examples given: "BK dentists," "Rolling Hills moms," "brick-and-mortar businesses," "salon owners," "retired athletes," "Brooklyn busy executives."
- **G — Give them a goal.** Name the prospect's dream outcome directly — a word, phrase, event, feeling, or result. The more specific and tangible, the stronger. Examples given: pain-free, celebrity smile, first place, never out of breath, double your profit, first client, 7-figure, $100,000.
- **I — Indicate a time interval.** State how long results will take, giving a concrete duration. **Explicit compliance caveat**: for quantifiable claims (income, weight loss), most ad platforms will reject copy that pairs a specific outcome with a specific timeframe, since that reads as an implied guarantee — this component should be used carefully or omitted where platform rules apply, but used freely where the "goal" isn't itself a quantifiable outcome claim (his contrast: "$10,000 in 10 days" — likely non-compliant — vs. "make your first sale in 10 days" — likely fine). Examples given: minutes/hours/days/weeks/months, "4-hour," "21-day," "6-week," "3-month."
- **C — Complete with a container word.** A word signaling the offer is a bundled system, not a single commoditized item — this is what makes it feel incomparable to a plain product/service. Examples given: challenge, blueprint, boot camp, intensive, incubator, masterclass, program, detox, experience, summit, accelerator, fast-track, shortcut, sprint, launch, system, transformation, mastermind, game plan, deep dive, workshop, comeback, hack, cheat code, liftoff.

**Two optional stylistic devices, explicitly framed as "nice to have," not requirements — don't force them:**

- **Rhyme** — his suggestion: use a rhyming dictionary as a shortcut. Examples given: "six-pack fast-track," "5-day book-print sprint," "marriage-thrive deep dive," "12-week gut shortcut."
- **Alliteration** — repeated starting sounds, which he notes is generally easier for most people to pull off than rhyming. Examples given: "make money masterclass," "change your life challenge," "big booty boot camp," "debt detox," "real estate reset."

**Fully assembled example names**, combining multiple MAGIC components (used to illustrate the formula in practice, not as literal reusable copy): "free 6-week lean by Halloween challenge," "88% off 12-week bikini blueprint," "free 21-day mommy makeover," "6-week stress release challenge," "90-day rapid healing intensive," "coaching 5 clients in 5 days blueprint," "fill your gym in 30 days free."

## Applying the same formula inside the offer, not just to the headline

**Pro tip Hormozi gives**: apply the MAGIC formula to *individual bonus items and sub-components* of the bundle, not just the top-level offer name — a well-named bonus automatically increases its own perceived value purely through naming, independent of anything else about it.

## What to change, in order, as an offer fatigues

Hormozi gives an explicit escalation sequence — always exhaust a lighter-weight change before moving to a heavier one, since each successive step is more operationally disruptive:

1. Change the creative (images/video in the ad).
2. Change the ad's body copy.
3. Change the headline/name (the "wrapping paper" — e.g., "free 6-week lean challenge" → "free 6-week tone challenge," or "holiday hangover" → "new year, new you").
4. Change the offer's stated duration (e.g., 6 weeks → 8 weeks).
5. Change the free/discount enhancer component.
6. Change the underlying monetization structure/pricing (explicitly flagged as a last resort, reserved for book two of the series — "money models").

His rationale for this ordering: in the overwhelming majority of cases, an ad going stale only needs the first one or two levels refreshed — a new image, a new hook — not a wholesale rebuild. Skipping straight to changing the core offer or monetization structure when a lighter fix would have worked introduces unnecessary operational drag and destroys the compounding value of an offer that's already been proven to convert. **Rule of thumb once an offer is actually working: rarely change it — "rinse and repeat over and over and over again."** He flags this as a genuine discipline problem for entrepreneurs specifically, since "we are entrepreneurs and love change," and that instinct toward novelty is, in this specific context, usually a cost rather than an asset.

## Local vs. national marketing tradeoff (context for why naming variation matters more locally)

Hormozi's stated view: local marketing is paradoxically both *easier* and *harder* than national marketing. Easier, because local trust ("trust in the familiar") makes in-person, higher-priced selling inherently more effective — leads convert at a higher rate. Harder, because a local business's addressable market (its "TAM," total addressable market — see the market-selection page) is capped by geographic radius, so the same audience gets reached repeatedly and offers fatigue much faster than at national scale. He credits learning to rapidly vary offer names, headlines, and creative while running local businesses as the skill that later made his transition to national-level advertising easier — the underlying discipline (vary presentation, don't touch the value stack) transfers directly.
