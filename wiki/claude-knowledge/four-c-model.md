## The two-layer model

The source material draws a consistent two-layer distinction:

- **Second Brain = knowledge.** Does the AI actually know what's going on in your business, your life, your relationships? Can you ask it questions and get real, accurate answers?
- **AI Operating System (AIOS) = action.** Once the knowledge layer exists, automation, skills, and agents get built on top of it that actually *do* things. This layer cannot exist without the second brain underneath it — action without a knowledge base to act on is just a disconnected script.

This splits into four parts, taken in **strict order** — the first two constitute the second brain itself; the second two constitute the operating-system layer built on top of it:

1. **Context** — who you are, who your business is, static/evergreen background. This is the actual substance of the second brain.
2. **Connections** — the ability to reach *live* data (message threads, financial dashboards, project-management tools) rather than storing static snapshots of things that constantly change. Static background goes in the brain directly; volatile data should be *reachable*, not duplicated into files that immediately start going stale.
3. **Capabilities** — skills, agents, automations, and pipelines built once context and connections already exist.
4. **Cadence** — capabilities running autonomously on their own (on a schedule or trigger) without being manually triggered and babysat every time. This is explicitly named as the *last* thing to build, and has to be **earned through demonstrated reliability**, not started with.

## The foundational mindset shift

An operating system doesn't start with architecture — it starts with a default. The shift the source material argues for is defaulting to working *inside* the AI coding agent itself (rather than juggling separate tool tabs, custom GPTs, or one-off subscriptions), so that context, memory, and preference all accumulate in one place over time instead of being scattered across tools that don't talk to each other.

## The gut-check test

If you asked your AI something about yourself or your business right now, would the answer sound like it came from a stranger — or from a teammate/co-founder who already knows what's going on? That's the practical test for whether the knowledge layer (Context + Connections) is actually doing its job, independent of whatever automation may or may not exist on top of it.

## Why order matters

The strict ordering (Context → Connections → Capabilities → Cadence) isn't incidental — it's the load-bearing part of the model. Capabilities built on top of a thin or unreliable knowledge layer inherit that unreliability; automation (Cadence) layered on top of unproven capabilities compounds whatever wasn't solid underneath. Skipping ahead — building automation before the knowledge layer is trustworthy — is the mechanism by which "an AI operating system" quietly becomes "an AI operating on bad information, faster."
