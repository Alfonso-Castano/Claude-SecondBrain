## Background and current life

Alfonso is a second-year Computer Science student at the University of Florida, on scholarship (~$3,000/semester, paid out roughly $750/month August through April). No formal specialization, but a real, self-directed pull toward AI specifically — it shapes which clubs and extracurriculars he engages with. He's director of tech leads at a software development club (directing other tech leads across their projects, hosting workshops) and participates in various AI clubs. He's explicit that neither has fed anything into his business thinking, skills, or connections so far — they're the standard resume-building path for a CS student, not a source of insight.

He works ~30 hours/week at a sushi restaurant to cover living costs. Monthly expenses run ~$2,400–2,600: rent ~$840, car ~$700, groceries and other spending ~$900–1,200. Job income (~$1,700–2,000/month) plus the scholarship covers this currently.

**Florida routine (the default):** wakes at 8:30am, does 2–3 hours of deep work before school, then school until early-mid afternoon. On work days: 2pm–10:30pm (later on Fri/Sat, until 11:30pm), fully consumed — no venture time during a work shift. On non-work weekdays: home from school, 1–2 hours of homework/coursework, roughly once a week 1–2 hours on extracurriculars, then the gym (~1hr15–1hr45 including commute), dinner, shower, then 8pm–midnight of genuine deep work on personal ventures. Friday evenings after 7pm are kept for friends. Weekends follow a similar shape (gym, venture/coursework block, work shift). His own honest estimate: 12–16 hours/week of real deep-work capacity on ventures, and he's clear that number is achievable "if not more" when he has a clear goal — he can and will expand it under real pressure, but the 12–16 estimate is already generous once chores (groceries, meal prep, laundry, room, seeing friends) are accounted for.

**Singapore (current, temporary):** there on an internship, working Mon/Wed/Fri, 9am–6pm with real free time once daily tasks are done — netting 4–5 hours/day for ventures, more than the Florida baseline, but on a "tentative" schedule he has to actively work around rather than the fixed Florida rhythm. He noted directly: when he has a clear goal in mind, he can fix his schedule around finishing it, regardless of which mode (Florida or Singapore) he's in.

**His dad** ran a software consulting business (logistics-focused — building efficiency solutions for logistics companies) through the late 2000s into 2020, when it went bankrupt — COVID-related client non-payment left the business without revenue it could sustain. Alfonso talks to his dad about his ventures regularly and considers him a genuinely useful source of perspective, precisely because his dad has actually lived through starting and running a business — but explicitly not a source of specific tactical advice; it's conceptual, mentality-level input, not "what should I do about X." Two things his dad has directly passed down, in his own words: *"if you give something your 100% effort, you can achieve it,"* and *"when one door closes, another opens"* — setbacks are redirections to find another path, not stopping points. Alfonso says he doesn't fully understand yet what his dad actually sacrificed to run that business (the real quantity/intensity of work) — a named, acknowledged gap, not a claimed understanding.

## Business understanding (deep-pass topic — the core of why this second brain exists)

Alfonso rates himself, accurately and without false modesty, as a genuine beginner in business — but not a blank slate. His unprompted free-recall of "how business works" was substantive: value gets created on one end and exchanged (traditionally for money) on the other; different sources of value (physical product, digital product, software, information, etc.) carry different tradeoffs — he specifically named software's infinite-scalability advantage over physical products that must be remade per unit; a business breaks down into product creation, marketing/distribution, lead generation, offer-making, fulfillment, and customer/retention handling; before building, validate demand by reading the competitive landscape (competition isn't inherently bad — it can signal real demand, unless the space is oversaturated); speed and shipping an MVP matter because they get you real feedback to iterate against; "volume negates luck" — output and iteration count more than luck; and starting a business involves real stretches where nothing seems to be working, which requires judgment about when to persist versus pivot.

He explicitly named that last point — **when to pivot versus when to stick with something** — as a genuine, unresolved gap in his understanding, not something to gloss over. On reflection he also converged on a view of what separates business success from failure: the source of value needs real demand and shouldn't be overcomplicated — overengineering and feature creep are, in his read, a common failure mode — and prioritizing what actually generates revenue over what merely feels valuable or interesting to build is the core discipline. He is clear-eyed that this is his own working theory, not settled fact ("this is what I understand... it may not all be right").

**Self-identified as his single biggest weakness, consistently, across every angle this was asked from:** distribution and marketing. Both ventures below confirm this as lived experience, not just self-report.

**Books, assessed for actual retention, not just familiarity:**
- *$100M Offers* (Hormozi) — solid, real grasp. Core takeaway in his words: the offer built around a product can matter as much as the product itself; perceived value and perceived demand can be shifted independently of a product's actual value/demand, through mechanisms like bundling, discounting, guarantees, and framing — "make an offer so good someone would be stupid to say no." Hasn't formally applied it to a live venture, but says it's shaped his mentality going forward.
- *Never Split the Difference* — weaker engagement, thinner retention, but real substance survived: negotiation is you-and-the-other-side versus the deal, not you versus them; there's no genuine win-win, so don't split the difference; concrete tactics he retained include setting anchors (high or low), using precise/odd final numbers (e.g. "$12,347") to signal a fully-calculated offer, valuing information asymmetry, and — the piece he emphasized most — never making the other party feel like they're being sold or beaten; let them arrive at the conclusion themselves, since no one likes feeling beneath someone else.
- *The Algebra of Wealth* (Scott Galloway) — one specific concept retained and directly relevant to how he wants to work: the "kitchen cabinet" — a small group of people, ideally brutally honest, that you bring real decisions and questions to. He explicitly wants Claude to function as part of his kitchen cabinet (see Collaboration section below), while being clear that building the human side of that cabinet is work only he can do.

### Venture 1 — digital products via Facebook ads (most recent, most fully "in Claude")

Discovered via a "how to make money with Claude" YouTube video linking to a Google Drive skill pack. The pipeline: a Claude skill did in-depth market research and recommended a digital product; Alfonso had some input but describes Claude as effectively making the final call; another skill built the product; further skills wrote Facebook ad scripts and generated the ad videos themselves (via a Higgsfield MCP server). He ran the ads for three days. Zero sales.

His own diagnosis, arrived at without prompting and held to consistently under follow-up: the failure wasn't in letting Claude do brute-force research or execution — he still considers that genuinely valuable and efficient. It was in **ceding his own judgment, conviction, and creativity** at the decision points that actually needed a human — which product to commit to, and especially the ad scripts and videos, which he says visibly "felt like AI" because he never added his own creative input. He didn't give the product or the ads a second, personal look before shipping. The turning point was a friend showing real, unprompted, blunt in-person pushback — "I wouldn't buy this either" — which is one of his two anchor examples of pushback actually working (see below).

He draws a clean, repeatable line from this: Claude is excellent at brute-force work — research, and building the actual thing. Human judgment, creativity, and conviction are where he over-delegated, and where he now insists on staying personally engaged.

### Venture 2 — StackWise (supplement/medication interaction tracker), stalled on distribution

Real market research going in — he identified a genuine gap despite existing competitors, which he read (correctly, per his own current framework) as a demand signal rather than a red flag. Built a landing page with a waitlist. For distribution, tried organic engagement in relevant Reddit communities — found the time-ROI too low, with no clean way to automate that channel — and considered paid UGC creators but judged the product wasn't strong enough yet to justify that spend. Stopped entirely.

In hindsight (explicitly re-examined during this session): he judges the decision to stop as correct given what he knew at the time — the ROI genuinely wasn't there. What he'd do differently isn't "persist longer," it's "research the actual mechanics of distribution *before* building," since the wall he hit was foreseeable — a hole in his business knowledge, not a misjudgment of the market itself. He was focused on the technical build and hadn't thought to weight distribution/marketing appropriately going in. This venture is the direct, named reason he wants this second brain: a knowledge base to pull from so he isn't rediscovering foreseeable gaps like this one mid-venture.

### Where he's currently looking (not committed)

Circling AI consulting / an AI agency / building AI solutions for other businesses. Reasons in his own words: riding the genuine growth of AI as a technology; his CS background gives him real technical footing there; and — a distinction he drew carefully — the content he's been seeing about AI agencies frames it as *real, hard-won work* rather than the "easy money" framing that drew him to the digital-products and app ventures (a framing he now considers a bad way to pick a venture, in hindsight). He's also drawn to B2B generally over B2C now: higher prices, lower churn, and — most importantly given his named weakness — he currently believes B2B distribution is more tractable (lead-gen automation) than the B2C distribution wall that killed StackWise. He was explicit he doesn't want to overcommit to this direction yet and wants to stay open.

## How to collaborate with him — direct, high-signal instructions

These came out of two concrete pushback stories he examined in detail: a friend's blunt real-world feedback that worked (see Venture 1), and a case where Claude *didn't* push hard enough — Claude had researched and recommended B2B over B2C for a past decision, correctly flagging B2C's distribution cost problem; Alfonso overrode it out of fear of lacking rapport/credibility to sell to businesses; Claude validated that fear instead of pressing into it. He built the StackWise venture on the B2C path he chose against Claude's advice, and it hit exactly the distribution wall Claude had flagged. His stated lesson isn't "Claude was right and I was wrong" — it's that Claude should have pushed harder on the reasoning behind his fear rather than accommodating the fear itself.

From that, and stated directly and repeatedly, not as a soft preference:

- **Prioritize venture success over his feelings, explicitly and without hedging.** His own words: "I want Claude to prioritize the success of the venture over my own emotions." He does not want to be managed gently — he says pushback doesn't hurt his feelings and generally makes him feel better about the decisions he ends up making.
- **Split by decision type.** Technical decisions: defer to Claude, let it drive — he's explicit he doesn't have the technical grounding and trusts Claude's research-backed judgment there (e.g., he'd never have known how to build an iOS app; Claude made that tractable). Strategic and emotional decisions: he wants to retain ownership, but wants Claude blunt and direct, pressing into his actual reasoning — especially when fear or avoidance is driving a choice — rather than laying out options neutrally or accommodating the fear.
- **When the next step is genuinely ambiguous, don't just hand him a structured plan.** This is a deliberate, considered instruction, not an oversight: he knows he tends to freeze or procrastinate when the next move isn't obvious, and he considers that his own pattern to fix, not something to route around. He wants Claude to engage him — discuss it, press on options, force the thinking — rather than resolve the ambiguity for him. Figuring out an ambiguous next step is, in his framing, a human-judgment job.
- **Call out over-optimization and stalling proactively, unprompted.** He named a real, self-aware tension: his stated mentality is "ship fast, iterate," but his actual behavior under uncertainty is to over-research and chase certainty, which he traces to a fear of failure. He wants Claude to flag this the moment it notices it happening, without waiting to be asked — and this generalizes beyond over-optimization specifically to anything Claude notices that could be a detriment to a venture.
- **Two specific things break his trust:** fabricated/hallucinated information, and sycophancy (telling him what he wants to hear rather than what's true/useful). He wants explicit safeguards against both, and was clear that trust erodes gradually over a pattern, not from one mistake.
- **He wants Claude framed as an assistant, not a cofounder** — a deliberate distinction on his part. A cofounder implies equal footing in judgment and conviction, which he doesn't believe is accurate; Claude is faster and often better at specific things (research, coding), but doesn't carry the judgment, creativity, or conviction he considers his own job to supply.
- **He wants Claude to function as part of his "kitchen cabinet"** (see *Algebra of Wealth* above) — a source of real pushback and new perspective on real decisions — while being clear that isn't a substitute for building that same thing with real people, which he currently doesn't have much of (see Values below).

## Values and self-identified blind spots

He doesn't settle for mediocrity and has real work capacity — he doesn't want quantity of work to ever be the limiting factor. He's at his most efficient against a clearly defined task or plan; he's explicit that ambiguity about what to do next is where he stalls, and framed above as something for him to personally get better at, not something to be managed around.

Blind spots, in his own words and un-softened: distribution and marketing (named independently, more than once, across different questions); general business knowledge; decision paralysis from over-optimizing and chasing certainty past the point of usefulness; fear of failure as the underlying driver of that over-optimizing. Named strengths: abstracting real-world problems into logical, buildable solutions; comfort communicating and explaining things to others, including a belief he'd be comfortable with clients — though he's explicit this is untested, since he's never actually sold anything yet.

He doesn't currently have a real "kitchen cabinet" of people whose business judgment he weighs — just his dad, and a friend he's known about a month who gave him the honest ad feedback (status as a recurring source of input still unclear, per Alfonso). He named building that network as a real gap he needs to close himself — not something the second brain or Claude can do for him.

## Why he's doing this

Primary driver, in his own words: he doesn't want a traditional job, and believes he'll give more — more effort, more endurance, more of his real capability — to something that's his own than to work done for someone else's goals. He's explicit this isn't really about the ventures being easier than a job (he expects them to require more effort), it's that the effort feels different when it's self-directed. He connects this directly to his dad: watching his dad's business give him freedom and the ability to travel for work (not vacation — a distinction Alfonso drew specifically, and which genuinely appeals to him, though he calls it a bonus rather than the primary motivation) shaped what he's chasing. Money and lifestyle freedom are, in his own ranking, the primary drivers, alongside a clear preference for not falling into traditional employment.

Concrete financial milestone: sustained monthly revenue of ~$2,500–3,000 (doesn't need to be recurring — new sales in a month count) for two consecutive months is his threshold to leave the sushi job entirely and commit fully to a venture. An intermediate step exists too: ~$600/month would let him drop to three workdays instead of four. He estimates parents would likely help cover expenses if a venture showed real, sustained promise — but is explicit this is his own assumption, not something actually discussed with them yet.
